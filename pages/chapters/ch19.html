GROUND COMBAT
Territory Battle Model · Cover · Deployment · Reserves · Design Vision — Chapter 19
Inspired by Dawn of War: Dark Crusade. Planetary Map → Territory Selection → Battle Map. Each territory is a PLACE with unique terrain. Procedural battle maps from biome-specific templates. Capital territories, resource nodes, and spaceports get unique hand-crafted elements.
Ground combat terrain is determined by planet type. Each of the 17 planet types creates distinct battlefield conditions — from the open killing fields of Dead Worlds to the corridor warfare of Orbital Stations. See Ch. 13 for detailed terrain profiles per planet type.
Ground Battle Flow

Territory Selection → Deployment → Auto-Battle → Resolution

Ground combat is territory-by-territory. Each planet is divided into territories (see Ch. 13); conquering a planet means taking territories until the defender has none left. The auto-battler handles unit behavior; the commander handles strategy. CP represents your commander’s attention — how many things one person can focus on at once (see Ch. 18 for the full CP system).
BATTLE FLOW
1. Territory Selection — Choose which territory to attack based on adjacency to your held territories. Options may be limited by your space battle performance (see Ch. 18). Multiple territories can be attacked simultaneously if you have enough generals (see Ch. 22).
2. Pre-Battle Setup — Assign a general to lead the assault. Set army-level formation (determines how squads auto-arrange). Configure Rules of Engagement. Review terrain intel from scanning. Assign forces from your available pool.
3. Deployment — Forces deploy at designated drop zones. Space superiority determines drop zone quality (see Ch. 18). Reserves staged behind the front line for triggered deployment.
4. Auto-Battle — Squads execute based on formation, ROE, and terrain. The general’s personality trait colors unit behavior (see Ch. 22). Commander intervenes with CP abilities, broad orders, and targeted squad redirects (squad redirect costs CP).
5. Resolution — Battle ends when victory conditions are met (see Variable Win Conditions below). Territory control changes. Forces resupply during the mandatory pause before the next engagement. After-action report with full replay available.
THE FORCED PAUSE
Between territory battles, there is a mandatory resupply pause. Your army regroups, heals (if supply lines are intact), and replenishes. During this pause, other factions and AI make their moves. This is the breathing room that makes the game turn-based at the strategic layer and real-time at the tactical layer. Units that are supply-blocked do NOT heal or resupply between battles.
Generals in Ground Combat

Command · Death · Morale Cascade

Generals aren’t abstract. They’re on the battlefield with their troops, and they can die. A general’s personality trait affects how their entire army fights (see Ch. 22). Losing a general mid-battle is catastrophic.
GENERAL ON THE FIELD
• General is attached to a command unit on the battlefield
• Their personality trait passively modifies all units under their command (see Ch. 22 trait bonuses)
• Their CP ability is available once per battle (see Ch. 22 trait→ability mapping)
• General gains XP from battles won, units killed, objectives completed
• Promotion path: Lieutenant → Captain → Colonel → General → Commander (see Ch. 22)
GENERAL DEATH & WOUNDING
• If the general’s command unit is destroyed, the general can die
• Survival chance based on rank + bodyguard quality + terrain: PH 30-70%
• Death: Permanent loss. All units under command suffer immediate morale shock. Army-wide PH -30% combat effectiveness for remainder of battle. Standing orders default to “hold position.”
• Wounded (survived): General extracted. Out of action for PH 3-5 turns. Army takes PH -15% morale penalty. Second-in-command assumes control with reduced bonuses.
Ground Rules of Engagement

Targeting · Aggression · Formation · Retreat — Your Standing Orders

Rules of Engagement work the same way on the ground as in space (see Ch. 18) but with ground-specific options. Set them before battle or spend CP to modify mid-fight. You can go fully autonomous or micromanage every squad’s behavior.
TARGETING PRIORITY
Default: Role-based (anti-armor targets vehicles, snipers target high-value). Player can set custom priority per squad type: “Focus infantry first,” “Ignore fortifications,” “Target the general.”
AGGRESSION
Assault — Push forward aggressively. Take ground. Accept higher casualties.
Balanced — Advance with cover-to-cover movement. Standard.
Defensive — Hold position. Dig in. Only fire when engaged.
General personality modifies interpretation (Aggressive general makes “balanced” more forward).
COVER USAGE
Aggressive cover — Use cover on advance but don’t stop to entrench
Standard — Move cover-to-cover, pause to establish position
Hunker — Find best available cover and stay. Maximum defense, zero advance.
SQUAD FORMATIONS
Army-level formation set by general (see Q27). Squads auto-adopt:
Defensive line → Squads spread, dig in, overlapping fields of fire
Aggressive push → Wedge formations, lead with heavy armor
Flanking maneuver → Split force, main body pins while flankers envelope
Fighting withdrawal → Leapfrog retreat, rear guard covers
RETREAT CONDITIONS
Never — Fight to the last. Desperate situations or Reckless generals.
At threshold — Squad retreats when below PH 30% strength
On order — Only when commander spends CP to order retreat
Smart retreat — Units fall back to better cover when taking heavy fire, re-engage when able
VEHICLE RULES
Spearhead — Vehicles lead, infantry follows in cover
Support — Vehicles hang back, provide fire support for advancing infantry
Independent — Vehicles pursue own targets based on role
Vehicle type determines terrain accessibility (see Ch. 13 planet types)
Variable Win Conditions

Territory Control · Timed Hold · Defense · Extraction — Context Determines Victory

TERRITORY CAPTURE
Standard. Eliminate or rout all enemy forces from the territory. Attacker controls the territory.
TIMED HOLD
Hold a specific position (objective point) for PH X minutes. Defender must dislodge attacker before timer expires.
DEFENSE
Survive an assault for PH X waves/minutes. Reinforcements incoming. Hold until relief arrives.
EXTRACTION
Protect a specific unit or asset while it evacuates. Win condition: asset reaches extraction point. Lose condition: asset destroyed.
HAZARD DEFENSE
Defend against planetary hazards — wildlife incursion, Vorax tendril ground assault, environmental catastrophe. Both armies may fight the hazard simultaneously (temporary ceasefire or three-way battle).
SIEGE
Assault a fortified position (bunker complex, planetary fortress, shield generator). Requires sustained bombardment + infantry push. Fortifications must be destroyed before objective can be captured.
Win conditions are communicated in the Pre-Battle Window. The auto-battle AI adapts unit behavior to the objective — in a Timed Hold, units will prioritize positioning on the objective rather than chasing kills.

Deployment & Reserves

Space Performance → Beachhead Size → Reserve Triggers

ATTACKER DEPLOYMENT
Decisive Victory: 3-4 territories wide beachhead. Standard (narrow): 2 territories. Pyrrhic (loss): 1 territory cramped. No space battle: 2 standard.
RESERVE TRIGGERS
Condition A: 25% casualties → reserves deploy at rear. Condition B: forward position captured → reserves deploy at captured point. Commit reserves early (more forces now) or hold (insurance later).

Morale & Routing

Faction Identity · Unit Personality · Breaking Points

Morale determines when units stop fighting. Every unit has a morale value that fluctuates during battle based on what’s happening around them. When morale breaks, the unit’s response depends on BOTH their faction AND their unit type. Morale is the invisible hand that makes battles feel alive — units don’t fight to the last man like robots (unless they ARE robots).
MORALE GAINS
• Winning nearby engagements
• General nearby (aura bonus, stronger with higher rank)
• Holding strong defensive position
• Reinforcements arriving
• Faction CP ability used (varies by faction)
• Destroying high-value enemy target
• Veterancy bonus (experienced troops hold longer)
MORALE LOSSES
• Taking heavy casualties quickly
• General killed or wounded
• Surrounded / flanked
• Supply cut off
• Friendly unit routing nearby (panic spreads)
• Under orbital bombardment
• Losing held objective
• Weather/environmental hazards
TERRAN LEAGUE
Infantry suppress in place (stop advancing, reduced fire rate, hunker in cover). Vehicles reverse to safer positions. Rally-capable — CP rally ability is most effective on Terrans. Stubborn under fire but crack when surrounded.
ETERNAL SHARDS
Scatter and regroup. Units break formation but attempt to reform at a rally point behind the line. Psychic link means morale loss spreads faster through nearby Shard units, but recovery is also faster when one unit rallies.
SCRAP-HORDE
Light units rout wildly (random direction, may run into enemy). Heavy units and berserkers go berserk — uncontrolled aggression, attack nearest target (friend or foe if too close). Meks ignore morale entirely (mechanical).
REVENANT
Do not rout. Instead, systems shut down sequentially. At critical morale threshold, units enter “conservation mode” — reduced combat effectiveness, slower, prioritize self-preservation over objective. Ancient programming prevents full retreat but also prevents berserk behavior. Predictable, exploitable.
UNITY ACCORD
Retreat in formation. Organized, disciplined withdrawal maintaining covering fire. The most orderly rout in the game — barely looks like a rout. However, once the formation breaks, individual Accord units surrender quickly (cultural aversion to pointless death).
FACTION-NEUTRAL NOTE
Unit TYPE also matters within each faction. Heavy armor holds longer than light scouts. Elite veterans hold longer than green recruits. Specialized units (snipers, engineers) have lower morale thresholds than line infantry. A Terran scout squad breaks before a Terran heavy weapons team.
Veterancy & Rank

Experience Shapes Behavior — Not Just Stats

Veteran units don’t just hit harder. They act smarter. The rank system (see Ch. 22) provides stat bonuses, but the real value of experience is in how units BEHAVE during the chaos of battle. A squad of green recruits with the same equipment as a veteran squad will lose — not because their guns are weaker, but because they panic, waste ammo on suppression, and break under pressure.
GREEN (Rank 0) Default AI behavior. Follow ROE literally. Poor cover usage (stand in the open too long). Panic fire under suppression (accuracy drops PH 40%). Break morale at PH 50% squad casualties. Slow to react to flanking.
EXPERIENCED (Rank 1-2) Better cover transitions. Suppress before advancing. Accuracy penalty under fire reduced to PH 20%. Break at PH 35% casualties. React to flanking within PH 3 seconds. Begin to coordinate with adjacent squads.
VETERAN (Rank 3-4) Cover-to-cover movement automatic. Focus fire on priority targets without ROE override needed. Accuracy stable under fire. Break at PH 20% casualties. Auto-retreat to better position when flanked. Coordinate flanking maneuvers with nearby veteran squads. Rally faster from morale breaks.
ELITE (Rank 5+) Near-perfect cover usage. Anticipate enemy movement (pre-aim likely positions). Minimal accuracy loss under any conditions. Break at PH 10% casualties or general death only. Auto-coordinate complex tactics (bounding overwatch, fire-and-maneuver). Presence boosts morale of nearby green units. These are the units that win battles single-handedly.
SQUAD VETERANCY
Veterans elevate the whole squad. A squad with PH 2+ veteran members performs one behavior tier above its average rank. One experienced soldier steadying a squad of green recruits makes them fight like they’ve been there before. This is why preserving veterans matters more than preserving equipment.
Terrain & Movement

Every Step Costs Something — Terrain Shapes the Battle

Terrain isn’t decoration. It determines movement speed, combat effectiveness, available cover, and vision range. Different terrain types interact with faction abilities and equipment loadouts (see Ch. 13 for full planet terrain profiles, Ch. 20 for equipment modifiers).
MOVEMENT
Open ground: full speed. Roads/paths: +PH 25% speed. Dense vegetation: -PH 40% speed. Rubble/ruins: -PH 30% speed. River/water: infantry wade (very slow), vehicles need bridge/ford. Elevation change: uphill slows, downhill speeds. Equipment modifiers: jump jets ignore terrain, treads handle mud, hover crosses water.
COMBAT MODIFIERS
Forest: +cover, -vision, -vehicle mobility. Urban ruins: +cover, tight sightlines, infantry advantage. Open desert: +range, +vision, -cover. Mountains: +height advantage, -mobility, +defense. Swamp: -speed, -accuracy (unstable footing), +concealment.
FACTION TERRAIN BONUSES
Soft advantages, not hard bonuses (see Ch. 13). Scrap-Horde moves normally through rubble/junk terrain. Revenant ignores ancient ruins hazards. Shards gain minor benefits in crystalline terrain. Terrans get supply bonuses near roads/infrastructure. Accord moves faster in natural terrain (forests, plains).
DESTRUCTIBLE TERRAIN
Cover can be destroyed. Trees blown down by explosives. Walls breached by heavy weapons. Bunkers collapsed by sustained fire. Destroying cover denies it to both sides. Orbital bombardment reshapes terrain (craters become new cover).
WEATHER EFFECTS
Rain: -accuracy, -visibility, +concealment. Sandstorm: -range, -shield effectiveness, reduced movement. Blizzard: -visibility (severe), -movement, cold debuffs on unshielded units. Volcanic eruption: area denial, random damage zones. Predictable weather from scanning (Ch. 13), sudden events mid-combat on all planet types.
EQUIPMENT TERRAIN INTERACTION
Jump jets: bypass terrain penalties, reach elevation quickly. Treaded vehicles: handle mud/rubble better than wheeled. Hover units: cross water, ignore ground obstacles. Sealed armor: ignore weather debuffs. Thermal gear: negate cold penalties. Each equipment choice matters based on WHERE you’re fighting.
Fog of War

Unit Vision · Terrain Modifiers · Intel Wins Battles

You can’t fight what you can’t see. Each unit has a sight radius that reveals the map around them. Unexplored territory is dark. Previously seen but currently unobserved areas show last-known enemy positions (which may be outdated). Fog of war rewards scouting, punishes blind pushes, and makes intel infrastructure (radar towers, sensor arrays) genuinely valuable.
UNIT VISION RANGES
• Scout units: PH 400 px (longest sight range)
• Infantry: PH 250 px standard
• Vehicles: PH 300 px (elevated sensors)
• Snipers: PH 350 px (scoped)
• General’s command unit: PH 200 px + intel overlay from infrastructure
TERRAIN VISION MODIFIERS
• Open desert/ice: +PH 50% vision range
• Forest/jungle: -PH 40% vision range
• Urban: variable (clear down streets, blind around corners)
• High ground: +PH 30% vision range
• Weather: rain -PH 15%, blizzard -PH 50%, sandstorm -PH 30%
INTEL INFRASTRUCTURE
Radar towers and sensor arrays built on held territory remove fog of war in their radius AND grant bonus CP regeneration (your commander can see more, so they can do more). Destroying enemy sensor infrastructure blinds them. Building your own is a priority in any ground campaign. Scouts can be sacrificed to reveal enemy positions for artillery/orbital strikes.
Reinforcements & Drop Zones

Auto-Deploy · CP Targeted Drops · Anti-Air Interception

Reserves don’t appear from nowhere. They deploy from designated drop zones based on your space superiority and transport capacity. The default is automatic deployment when triggered, but spending CP lets you place reinforcements exactly where you need them. The enemy gets a say too — anti-air defenses and interceptor units can damage reinforcements before they land.
AUTO-DEPLOY (DEFAULT)
Reserves deploy automatically at pre-set drop zones when:
• A squad is wiped out (replacement triggered)
• The battle reaches a phase threshold
• The commander’s standing orders call for reinforcement
Drop zone locations determined by beachhead from space battle (see Ch. 18). Automatic, no CP cost, but predictable — the enemy knows where your reserves will land.
CP TARGETED DROP
Spend PH 2 CP to choose WHERE reinforcements land. Any location within your control radius or adjacent contested territory. Bypasses default drop zones. Useful for flanking drops, reinforcing a collapsing line, or surprise assaults. Higher risk — dropping into contested territory means units arrive under fire.
ANTI-AIR INTERCEPTION
Enemy anti-air defenses and interceptor units can target incoming drops:
• Light AA: PH 10-15% casualties on drop
• Heavy AA: PH 25-35% casualties on drop
• Fighter interception: PH 15-20% casualties
Destroying enemy AA before dropping reinforcements is a priority. Orbital suppression fire (if space superiority held) can suppress AA during drops.
SPACE SUPERIORITY GATE
No space control = no reinforcements. If you lose your fleet mid-ground-campaign (see Ch. 18), your ground forces are cut off. No drops, no resupply, no extraction. They fight with what they have until you retake orbital control or they’re destroyed. This is why space battles matter even if your goal is on the ground.
Retreat & Withdrawal

CP Cost · Physical Withdrawal · Rear Guard · Consequences

Retreat is a command decision, not a button press. It costs CP to order (your commander’s attention), and your units must physically withdraw under fire. A well-executed retreat preserves your army for the next battle. A botched one turns withdrawal into massacre.
RETREAT FLOW
1. Order Retreat — Commander spends PH 3 CP. All units receive withdrawal order. ROE automatically shifts to “defensive/fall back.”
2. Rear Guard — Highest-morale units auto-designated as rear guard (or player assigns via CP). Rear guard holds position to cover withdrawal. These units take the heaviest casualties.
3. Withdrawal — Units fall back toward drop zone / extraction point. Movement speed based on terrain. Units under fire move slower. Vehicles can cover infantry retreat.
4. Extraction — Units reaching extraction point are saved. Units that don’t make it are lost (killed or captured). General extracted with command unit (if alive).
SUCCESSFUL RETREAT
Army preserved (minus rear guard losses). Falls back to last held territory on the planet. Can resupply and re-engage next turn. General survives with experience.
FAILED RETREAT / NO RETREAT
If CP is unavailable for retreat order, units fight until routed (faction-specific morale break). Routed units scatter — some escape, some are captured, some die. If no territory to fall back to, units are lost entirely. Losing your last territory = losing the planet.
Fortification & Defenses

Turrets · Bunkers · Minefields · Shield Generators

Defenders can build fortifications on held territories between battles. Fortifications take time and resources but dramatically increase defensive strength. They’re also primary targets for orbital bombardment (see Ch. 18) and siege-type battles.
TURRETS
Automated weapon emplacements. Fire on enemies within range without CP cost. Various types: anti-infantry, anti-vehicle, anti-air. Can be destroyed by focused fire or bombardment. Placement matters — covering approaches and chokepoints.
BUNKERS
Infantry garrison points. Provide heavy cover (+PH 60% damage reduction) and firing positions. Can be destroyed by heavy weapons or sustained fire. Garrisoned infantry fights at reduced mobility but massively increased survivability.
MINEFIELDS
Area denial. Damage and slow units that enter. Can be cleared by engineers or detonated by expendable units. Invisible until triggered (unless enemy has scanning). Placement during the fortification phase, not during battle.
SHIELD GENERATORS
Area shields that reduce incoming damage for all friendly units within radius. High value target — destroying the generator removes protection for everything it covers. Requires power supply (ties to planetary infrastructure).
WALLS & BARRIERS
Physical barriers that block movement and provide cover. Must be breached by heavy weapons, engineers, or orbital strike. Channeling enemy movement through kill zones. Different materials have different HP (concrete, energy barrier, scrap-built).
SENSOR TOWERS
Remove fog of war in radius. Grant CP regeneration bonus. Detect incoming enemy forces and reinforce drops. High priority target for attackers. Can be built in forward positions for early warning.
BUILD RULES
Fortifications are built during the resupply pause between battles, not during combat. Build time and cost depend on fortification type and territory infrastructure. Better-developed territories support more fortifications. Maximum fortification slots per territory: PH 6-10 based on territory size.
After-Action Report

Battle Summary · XP Awards · Promotions · Replay

Every battle generates a detailed after-action report. This isn’t just a score screen — it’s how you learn, improve, and track your army’s growth. The replay system lets you rewatch any battle at any speed to analyze what went right and what went wrong.
CASUALTY REPORT
Per-squad breakdown: units lost, units wounded, units captured. Enemy casualties confirmed. Friendly fire incidents (if any). Equipment lost vs recovered. Total force strength before and after.
XP & PROMOTIONS
XP awards per squad based on: kills, objectives held, survival, special actions. General XP from battle outcome. Promotions highlighted with new rank and unlocked abilities. MVP unit identified (highest contribution score).
SALVAGE & RECOVERY
Equipment recovered from battlefield. Enemy equipment captured. Wounded units’ recovery timeline. Replacement units needed and availability.
STRATEGIC IMPACT
Territory control change. Planet conquest progress (X of Y territories held). Supply line status. Next available actions (push forward, consolidate, withdraw).
BATTLE REPLAY
Full battle replay available at 1x, 2x, 4x speed. Camera follows your perspective or can be set to free-cam. Useful for: identifying unit performance issues, learning enemy AI patterns, finding optimal CP timing, reviewing ROE effectiveness. Replays persist until the end of the campaign.
§19b.1 — Design Inspirations

Three Games · One System

DAWN OF WAR: DARK CRUSADE
Territory conquest map. Garrison defense. Attacker deploys from map edge. Buildings give defensive bonuses. Every territory is a place with meaning, not just a hex. The weight and permanence of conquest.
STARCRAFT
Unit composition and counter-play. Rock-paper-scissors dynamics. Positioning matters. The satisfaction of building the right army for the right fight. Visual readability at a glance.
MECHABELLUM
Auto-battler deployment. Formation strategy. Reserve waves. Player sets up, then watches with surgical intervention. The commander fantasy — preparation IS the skill expression.
§19b.2 — The Blend

How Each Contributes to Ground Combat

Pre-Battle: Deploy units in formation (Mechabellum) at a territory (Dark Crusade)
During Battle: Units auto-fight (Mechabellum) with counter-play mattering (StarCraft)
Terrain: Defender has building bonuses from territory (Dark Crusade)
Decisions: Player picks composition + formation + reserves, then watches. CP abilities for surgical intervention.
NON-NEGOTIABLE: AUTO-BATTLER STAYS
The auto-battler core is the foundation of the entire combat experience. Players do NOT directly control units. The skill expression is in preparation (80%) and intervention (20%). This is what makes Ashes of Command a strategy game, not a tactics game.
