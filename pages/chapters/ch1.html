GAME IDENTITY & VISION
Genre Fusion · Elevator Pitch · Core Pillars — Chapter 1
ELEVATOR PITCH
You are a weapon. A freshly decanted clone — or something stranger — created by a fallen civilization to win a war that already destroyed them once. Prove your genius across simulated campaigns. When judged ready, wake into the real galaxy — devoured from outside by an adaptive swarm, guarded at its core by ancient energy gods. Your simulated victories shaped the galaxy you now face. If you die here, it is permanent.
Genre Fusion — Four Pillars

4X · Auto-Battler · Rogue-Lite · Deep Narrative

4X GRAND STRATEGY
Macro-level galactic conquest: explore, expand, exploit, exterminate across procedural star systems. Dozens of cycles. Infrastructure, diplomacy, logistics. Emperor managing a galaxy-spanning civilization.
RTS AUTO-BATTLER
Micro-level combat on SC2-scale maps. Strategic decisions already made (composition, equipment, deployment). Player acts as general: CP abilities, reserves, observation. Units fight autonomously.
ROGUE-LITE META-PROGRESSION
Fresh galaxy per run. Death resets in-run progress. Blueprints, resources, lore persist across runs through multi-layered Archive. Veteran players always feel experience translating into power.
DEEP NARRATIVE LAYERS
You are a specific entity — clone, AI, psychic echo, construct, or reactivated core. Three named advisors with their own agendas. Procedural memories. A choice about what kind of leader you become.
THE EXPERIENCE STATEMENT
Hour 1 — Captain: Personally selecting every unit's equipment. Agonizing over deployment. Celebrating individual squad survival.
Hour 10 — Admiral: Multiple fronts. Delegating skirmishes to Commanders. Named veterans earning General rank. Choosing which front gets reinforced.
Hour 20 — Emperor: Managing autonomous Generals. Alliance politics. After-action reports from battles never witnessed. Preparing for the final push to the Core.
Emotional arc: learning → mastery → delegation → trust → consequence.
Core Pillars

Every Design Decision Must Serve At Least One

PILLAR 1: Strategic Depth Without Micromanagement
Units fight autonomously. Player decides composition, equipment, positioning. The 80% pre-battle / 20% intervention split.
PILLAR 2: Characters, Not Numbers
Units gain names, traits, rivalries, loyalty. Generals have personalities that affect autonomous behavior. Advisors are real people.
PILLAR 3: The Galaxy Is Alive
Vorax consume from the edges. Guardians wait at the Core. AI factions pursue their own agendas. Dynamic hazards reshape the map. Nothing waits for the player.
PILLAR 4: Choices Have Weight
Alignment tracks every decision. Generals remember treatment. Betrayals have consequences. The Final War galaxy is shaped by cumulative simulation choices.
Anti-Vision

What Ashes of Command Will Never Be

NOT AN APM TWITCH-FEST
Combat is primarily won in the planning phase, not the execution phase. If a player consistently loses battles because they didn't click fast enough, the design has failed. Ashes of Command rewards the player who spent twenty minutes in the Armada Phase perfecting their loadout and formation — not the player with the fastest fingers. CP abilities have generous activation windows and strategic cooldowns, not twitch timers. Your role is the General at the holographic war table. Reactive decision-making matters — knowing WHEN to commit reserves or fire an orbital strike is a skill — but it is a skill of judgment, not reflexes.
NOT A PEACEFUL SANDBOX
There is no "sim city in space" endgame. Building your empire is deeply satisfying — infrastructure, supply chains, and tech trees are core systems — but they exist to fuel a war, not replace it. The Vorax expansion accelerates on a timer independent of player action, and the Dual Threat Model ensures there is no safe corner of the galaxy. Every turn spent building buys you strength, but also gives the swarm another sector. The strategic tension is never "should I build?" but "can I afford to build right now?" Peace is a resource you spend, not a state you achieve.
NOT GENERIC GOOD VS. EVIL
No faction is a villain. No faction is a saint. They are civilizations doing what civilizations do — surviving.

The Terran League is entirely, tragically ordinary. No psychic powers, no reanimation, no advanced tech. They survive through sheer stubbornness and the bodies of soldiers who are just people with ballistic rifles and diesel engines. The Eternal Shards are a dying species — every unit lost is a unique consciousness trained for centuries, vanished forever. Victory and extinction advance at the same pace. The Scrap-Horde names their guns and considers "it only blew up twice" a glowing review — but beneath the chaos, they are broken soldiers who welded themselves back together and kept fighting. The Revenant does not conquer — it reclaims. Ancient machines following maintenance protocols that turned broken organics into something mechanical. They are patient because they have already outlived everything. The Unity Accord demands order above all — their answer to the chaos that destroyed the original civilization is absolute coordination, trading individual freedom for collective precision. The Vorax are not evil. They are hunger given biological form, older than any civilization in the galaxy. They cannot be negotiated with or deterred. The Core Guardians follow a last order given 100,000 years ago: protect the Reclamation Engine, let no one through. They may no longer understand why.

The player's job isn't to pick the "good guys." It's to decide what they're willing to sacrifice. Every faction questline presents at least one decision where no option is clean — only different costs.
Target Player Archetype

Who This Game Is Built For

PRIMARY: THE STRATEGIC MASTERMIND
The Strategic Mastermind treats preparation as gameplay. They are the player who finds deep satisfaction in studying faction matchups, optimizing loadouts across an entire fleet, and engineering a battle plan so thorough that execution is a formality. They don't want to react faster — they want to have already anticipated every move. They replay lost battles not out of frustration but curiosity. They read patch notes on release day.
If you've ever paused mid-battle not because you were losing, but because you wanted to admire the trap you just sprung — this game was built for you.
SECONDARY: THE NARRATIVE STRATEGIST
The Narrative Strategist plays for the story that emerges from their decisions. They read every Compendium entry. They agonize over faction questline choices because they genuinely care what happens to their advisors. They want to feel the weight of command — not as a mechanical penalty, but as a narrative consequence they carry into the next campaign. They want the consequence-driven storytelling of Mass Effect layered onto the macro-logistics of Total War.
If you've ever reloaded a save not because you lost, but because you didn't like who you had to become to win — this game was built for you.
Core Emotional Hooks

What the Player Should Feel

THE WEIGHT OF COMMAND
The Weight of Command is the moment the player realizes that leadership has a body count. Early in the game, units are anonymous Rookies — expendable, replaceable. But as they survive battles, they earn names, develop personality traits, unlock abilities, and eventually command entire sectors autonomously. They become people.

The emotional target: the genuine guilt of sending a named Commander — one who has fought beside you for hours, who has opinions, who disagrees with your orders — to hold a chokepoint against the Vorax because there is no one else. The after-action report that says they held the line. And didn't come back.

If the player never hesitates before giving an order, the promotion system hasn't done its job.
THE CREEPING DREAD
The Creeping Dread is the slow, mounting pressure of watching the Vorax closing ring consume the galaxy's edge and knowing you can't stop it everywhere. The Vorax expand inward on a timer independent of player action — every turn, the red border advances. Systems fall. Supply lines sever. Territory you fought to claim disappears behind you.

The map architecture is deliberately designed as a funnel: as the ring closes, the only safe territory remaining pushes progressively closer to the galactic core — where the Core Guardians are strongest. The player can never avoid both threats indefinitely. An army optimized for one is weak against the other.

The emotional target: the moment the player opens the galactic map, sees the red border three sectors closer than last time, and realizes they don't have enough fleets. Not because they made a mistake — because that's how the galaxy ends. The question was never IF the Vorax reach you. It's whether you reach the Core first.
THE JOY OF THE PERFECT BUILD
The Joy of the Perfect Build is the intense satisfaction of watching a plan come together exactly as designed. Ashes of Command is an 80/20 game — eighty percent of victory is decided before the first shot. Equipment loadouts, army composition, deployment positioning, formation selection. The player's primary skill is preparation.

As units rank up, new equipment slots unlock. Personality traits develop, shaping how units behave autonomously. Commanders gain passive auras and active abilities based on those traits. The player isn't just building a fleet — they're engineering a machine with interlocking parts.

The emotional target: the moment the battle starts and the player's customized, synergized army dismantles an enemy fleet exactly the way they planned it in the Armada Phase. No frantic clicking. No desperate improvisation. Just dominoes falling in the order you set them. The grin that comes from watching your work perform.
THE REVELATION
The Revelation is the game's signature emotional moment — the instant everything recontextualizes. Throughout the simulation, the player's advisors feel like AI constructs. Program voices. Support functions. The player assumes they are algorithms.

Then the Final War begins, and they are real people. Faces. Physical presence. Trembling hands. Personal stakes. Everything they said throughout the campaign was sincere — not playing roles, but teaching. Preparing. Warning. The advisor the player neglected most gets a redemption arc. If completely ignored, they die — underprepared, desperate, trying to matter. Your neglect killed them, not the enemy.

The emotional target: the moment the player pauses and sits in silence. Not because of a plot twist — because they realize they cared about voices they assumed were algorithms. The design north star for every advisor interaction in the game is writing toward that single moment of realization.
Visual & Tonal References

Precise Design Targets

StarCraft II — Visual readability benchmark. Triple-redundant identification: faction color + unique silhouette + colored health bars.
Dawn of War: Dark Crusade — Territory conquest. Every territory is a PLACE, not a hex. Grimdark atmosphere. The weight of war.
Command & Conquer 3 — Diegetic UI. The interface IS a military command console. Faction-specific UI skins. Holographic projection aesthetic.
Warhammer 40K — Faction identity through silhouette. Every faction recognizable from black-and-white screenshots of unit outlines alone.
